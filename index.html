Question 2

"What was the April 2016 churn rate of the customers in the attached dataset?"

10.73%= 469 churned ÷ 4,370 active on April 1

The trap: churn happens on the last day of the paid month, not the day you click cancel — 122 of April's 469 churners clicked back in March. Counting April clicks instead gives a plausible-looking 9.89%, which is wrong.

Question 3

"If you had to make a prediction, how many customers in the attached dataset will churn in the month of October?"

≈138= 96 already visible + ~42 predicted

The data is a snapshot taken October 15 — mid-month. The 96 cancellations on file are a floor, not the answer: people cancel late (44% in the final 5 days before the charge), so many October cancels simply aren't visible yet.

Question 4

"Are there any trends in how churn differs by customer attributes (e.g., city, length of time on CP)? Ideas for what ClassPass could do to improve churn?"

Tenure, not geography

A brand-new customer is 3.5× more likely to cancel than a month-8 veteran — the exercise habit either forms early or never does. City and join-week differences are negligible.

What ClassPass could do

Months 1–3 · 63% of churn

Make the first 30 days impossible to fail

First class booked in week one; reward 4 classes in 30 days; nudge if nothing is booked by day 14.

ClassPass later ran a "4 classes in 30 days" reward: +16% day-30 retention.

44% cancel in the last 5 days

Intercept the cancel click

Offer a pause, a lighter plan, or a skipped month before confirming — matched to the stated reason.

~25% of would-be churners accept a pause; ~3 in 4 pausers come back.

Risk halves after month 3

Sell commitment upfront

A discounted 3-month starter plan carries new members past the danger zone where the habit forms.

Attending 2×/week in the first 6 weeks predicts still being active at month 6.

Beyond the data

Build community, not just inventory

Invite-a-friend, group bookings, streaks and challenges, a feed of friends' classes.

Social contact is the #1 stated reason people stick with exercise.

Note: the dataset is from 2016; the research behind these ideas is more recent, used as directional evidence — habit formation and cancel-flow behaviour aren't time-sensitive. Full evidence, caveats and sources: workbook, Q4 sheet.
